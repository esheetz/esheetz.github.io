I have participated in a number of research projects throughout my undergraduate career, including two Research Experiences for Undergraduates (REUs). For more details on my responsibilities during these research projects, see my resume. My primary research interests include autonomous systems and artificial intelligence.

Monmouth College

SOfIA 2014

During the summer of 2014, I participated in the Summer Opportunities for Intellectual Activity (SOfIA) project, a three week research experience through Monmouth College focused on introducing freshmen to research. I worked on the High Speed Imagery and Mathematical Modelling project, in which we modelled the popping of a soap bubble.

Independent Research

Particle Image Velocimetry

During the 2014 - 2015 school year, I conducted independent research in the high speed imagery lab under the supervision of Dr. Michael Sostarecz. I used particle image velocimetery (PIV) to explore water flow around an airfoil at various angles of attack.

SOfIA 2015

During the summer of 2015, I participated in the SOfIA program as a returning student mentor for the High Speed Imagery and Mathematical Modelling project. The group explored applications of TEMA tracking software and researched fundamentals of chaos theory.

Honors Capstone

Chaos Theory and Free Will

The culmination of the Monmouth College Honors Program is an interdisciplinary capstone project. With the help of supervisors Dr. Michael Sostarecz and Dr. Hannah Schell, I researched and integrated topics in the mathematics of chaos theory and philosophical questions of free will. My capstone project involved an oral presentation and a final paper.

University of Arizona

CAT Vehicle REU

I spent the summer of 2016 at the University of Arizona in Tucson working with the Cognitive Autonomous Test (CAT) Vehicle REU. My research partner and I used system identification methods to create a mathematical model to represent the internal dynamics of the CAT Vehicle's controller. By the end of the ten week program, we had found a model for the velocity controller in the vehicle and demonstrated our model on a Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) controller. Our research is explained in our paper draft. As part of the program, my fellow REU participants and I put together videos to describe our experiences. My video is embedded below.
